**Short version:** Two systems decide whether a bank lets you open an account: **ChexSystems** and **Early Warning Services (EWS)**. Some banks pull them and deny hard. Some barely look. And a handful of fintechs don't check ChexSystems at all. If you've been denied — or you're churning bonuses and don't want to get locked out — this is the map of who's lenient, who's strict, and how to stay clean.

A quick honesty note before the lists: **no bank publishes its denial rules.** They change quietly. So I've split everything below into what's *confirmed* (the bank co-owns the system, the report is free by law) versus what's *community-reported* (which banks deny and how hard). Treat the second kind as a starting point, not gospel — and always pull your own reports first.

## The two systems, in one breath

- **ChexSystems** — a consumer reporting agency (owned by Fiserv) that tracks deposit-account trouble: forced account closures, unpaid negative balances, returned checks, and inquiries. Used very broadly across the industry. Negative items stay **5 years**.
- **Early Warning Services (EWS)** — a separate reporting agency that is **co-owned by seven of the largest banks**: Bank of America, Capital One, JPMorgan Chase, PNC, Truist, U.S. Bank, and Wells Fargo. It also runs Zelle. Because it's a shared consortium, **one bad mark can hurt you at all seven owner banks at once.** Negative items generally stay **5 years** (up to 7 for serious fraud under the FCRA).

A clean ChexSystems file does **not** mean a clean EWS file. A bank can check one, the other, or both. That's the whole reason people get blindsided.

## Banks that don't use ChexSystems (the lenient list)

These are the safest places to start if you've got a negative mark or you've been denied. **Most of these are fintechs**, not chartered banks — banking is provided by an FDIC partner bank — but the "no ChexSystems" part is real.

| Name | What it is | ChexSystems? | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Chime** | Fintech (The Bancorp / Stride Bank) | No ChexSystems, no credit check to open | Widely reported |
| **Varo** | Actual chartered bank | No ChexSystems, no credit check to open | Widely reported |
| **Current** | Fintech (Choice Financial / Cross River) | No ChexSystems on application | Widely reported |
| **GO2bank** | Green Dot Bank product | No ChexSystems review | Widely reported |
| **SoFi** | Fintech bank | Reported to not require a ChexSystems pass to open | Widely reported |
| **Capital One 360** | Chartered bank | Reported lenient — does not appear to deny standard 360 accounts on ChexSystems | **Uncertain — hedge** |

**Two corrections people get wrong constantly:**

- **Discover is NOT on this list.** Discover Bank *does* use ChexSystems and *can* deny you for negative history. A lot of "no ChexSystems" listicles get this wrong. Don't count on it.
- **Capital One 360** is genuinely murky. Some sources say it stopped using ChexSystems years ago; others say it may soft-pull or reference it. It's *lenient*, but I won't promise it ignores ChexSystems entirely. If you have a clean-ish record, it's a strong bet. If you're badly flagged, don't bank on it.

## Banks that DO use ChexSystems / EWS and will deny you

This is where the bonuses are biggest — and the screening is real. The big banks below pull ChexSystems and/or EWS as standard onboarding.

- **Chase** — pulls ChexSystems on most products. A prior Chase closure with a negative balance is close to an automatic denial. (Note: **Chase Secure Banking** skips ChexSystems but uses **EWS** instead — it's a second-chance account, not a no-screening one.)
- **Wells Fargo** — standard ChexSystems screening. Second-chance product is **Clear Access Banking**.
- **Bank of America** — ChexSystems as standard onboarding.
- **U.S. Bank, PNC, Truist, Capital One** — the other EWS owner banks; expect EWS screening on top of any ChexSystems check.
- **Citi** — reported to be **inquiry-sensitive**: community datapoints suggest denials after roughly 9–10 new-account inquiries in a few months. Your *negative history* may matter less here than your recent *application velocity*.

> **Why "inquiry-sensitive" matters for churners:** every application usually creates an inquiry. A pile of recent inquiries reads like fraud risk to some banks' models, even with a spotless history. Inquiries fade in relevance after about 12 months.

There are also long lists of **inquiry-sensitive credit unions** floating around the churning community (Byline, BECU, Bank of Hope, and others with "deny over N inquiries in M months" rules). I'm deliberately not reprinting specific thresholds here because the canonical list is years old and almost certainly stale. If you want, I'll rebuild a *current* inquiry-sensitivity list from fresh datapoints — that's its own piece.

## The consortium trap (the part most people miss)

Because EWS is **owned by seven big banks and pools their data**, an "account abuse" flag — a charge-off, an unpaid negative balance, a forced closure at *one* of them — is visible to *all* of them. Burn Chase by walking away from a negative balance, and you may quietly find Bank of America, Capital One, PNC, Truist, U.S. Bank, and Wells Fargo harder to get into too.

That's the single most important reason to **never close a bonus account while it's negative.** It's not "one bank mad at you." It's potentially seven doors closing at once.

## The playbook: stay clean and get approved

1. **Pull both reports before you start.** ChexSystems and EWS are each free by law. Do it once a year, and always after a denial.
   - ChexSystems: chexsystems.com, or call **800-428-9623**. (Free Consumer Disclosure under the FCRA.)
   - Early Warning Services: request your file disclosure at earlywarning.com, or call **1-800-745-1560**. (Free — EWS does not charge for your own report.)
2. **Bring every account to $0 or positive before you close it.** Never let one force-close. This is the flag that propagates across the consortium.
3. **Space out applications.** A rough community heuristic is ~10 or fewer new accounts a year, and don't cluster a bunch in a short window — especially at inquiry-sensitive banks.
4. **Hold accounts past the bonus terms** so you don't trip early-closure tracking.
5. **Dispute real errors.** Both agencies must reinvestigate, generally within ~30 days. If a denial cites a report, you're entitled to a free copy and the name of the agency used (adverse-action rights under the FCRA).
6. **If you're flagged, start with the lenient list** above to keep earning while negative items age off (5 years), or use a true second-chance account to rebuild.

## Beyond Chex and EWS (so you're not surprised)

Two more screening systems occasionally come up:
- **LexisNexis Risk Solutions** — identity and risk verification used in onboarding; free consumer disclosure available.
- **TeleCheck / Certegy** — check-acceptance and returned-check risk, more relevant at merchants than at account opening.

You don't need to obsess over these, but if a denial notice names one of them, now you know what it is and that you can pull it free.

### FAQ (for FAQPage schema)
- **What banks don't use ChexSystems?** As of mid-2026, Chime, Varo, Current, GO2bank, and SoFi are widely reported not to check ChexSystems to open an account; Capital One 360 is lenient but less certain. Note most of these are fintechs using a partner bank.
- **Does Discover use ChexSystems?** Yes. Despite appearing on some "no ChexSystems" lists, Discover Bank uses ChexSystems and can deny on negative history.
- **Will one bad mark really block me at multiple banks?** It can. Early Warning Services is co-owned by seven large banks and shares data, so an account-abuse flag from one can affect approvals at the others.
- **How do I get my Early Warning Services report?** Free at earlywarning.com or by calling 1-800-745-1560. It's a separate report from ChexSystems — pull both.
- **How many bank accounts can I open per year safely?** There's no official rule, but a common community guideline is around 10 or fewer, spaced out, with the bigger risk being negative items (forced closures, unpaid balances) rather than inquiries.
