**The short version:** A handful of business credit cards earn strong flat rewards *and* give you 0% APR on purchases for the first 12 months. Put your spending on one, leave your actual cash in a high-yield account earning ~4.5%, and pay the card off right before the 0% ends. You earn the rewards, you earn the interest, and the bank floats your spending for free. Done right — welcome bonus + flat rewards + float interest — it works out to **~7% back on everything**, and you can rotate a stable of these cards to keep it running almost indefinitely.

## Why a flat 2% card becomes "7% back"

Take the **Amex Blue Business Plus**: 2x Membership Rewards on every purchase (up to $50k/year), no annual fee, and **0% APR for 12 months**. The 7% is three things stacked:

1. **The welcome bonus** — 15,000–75,000 MR depending on the offer, spread across your spend.
2. **2x MR on everything** — worth ~2% as cash, more via transfer partners.
3. **The float** — instead of paying the card monthly, you keep that cash in a 4.5% high-yield account for up to a year and pocket the interest.

Real example: on **$12,282 of spend**, that's ~50,000 MR (including the bonus) + ~$430 of float interest = **$926, or 7.54%** — about **7.0% after the tax on the interest.** Even at a modest $3,000 of spend with the standard 15,000-point bonus, it still lands near 7%.

## How the 0% float actually works

Normally you pay a credit card in full every month, because 18–25% interest would wipe out any rewards. But during a 0% intro period you can do the opposite:

1. **Spend normally** on the card.
2. **Set autopay to the minimum** (usually ~1% of the balance or $40).
3. **Keep your real cash in a high-yield savings account** earning ~4.5% — the bank is floating your purchases interest-free.
4. **Mark your calendar** for the 0% end date (it's on the bottom of every statement).
5. **Pay the balance in full a few days before** that date.

You earn rewards on the spend *and* interest on the cash you didn't have to hand over yet.

## Why business cards (and why it doesn't hurt your credit)

This works best with **business** cards for two reasons. First, Amex **soft-pulls** you for a new business card once you have a relationship — so every ~6 months you can re-apply for the Blue Business Plus or Blue Business Cash, **reset the 0% clock, and grab a fresh welcome bonus** without a hard inquiry. Second, **most business cards don't report to your personal credit**, so carrying a large balance during the float doesn't dent your score.

> ⚠️ Two exceptions to know: **Capital One business cards report to personal credit** (and no longer offer a 0% intro) — don't use them for this. And many small **regional/credit-union** business cards don't disclose their reporting — verify before you lean on one.

## Can you really run this "indefinitely"?

Close. The **Amex soft-pull loop** is the engine you can run every ~6 months. Chase Ink, U.S. Bank, and Wells Fargo each add a 12-month window plus a bonus (those are hard pulls, so space them out). And the regional/credit-union cards give you a long tail of extra 0% windows to stack on the end. The honest caveat: those regional cards mostly earn little — they're there for the **float**, not the rewards.

## Don't do this if…

- You can't pay the balance in full before the 0% ends (the interest will erase everything).
- You don't have a high-yield savings account to hold the float.
- You carry credit-card debt or struggle to budget — this gives the bank money, not you.

## FAQ

**Do I need a business to get these cards?** You qualify as a sole proprietor if you earn any side income — apply with your SSN. (See our [business bank account guide](/blog/business-bank-bonuses-no-business-2026).)

**Does carrying a balance during the 0% period hurt my credit?** With Amex, Chase, and U.S. Bank business cards, the balance doesn't report to personal credit. Capital One business cards *do* report — avoid those for this.

**Is "7% back" really sustainable?** The float interest portion shrinks as you approach the payoff date, which is why you reset with a new card roughly every 6 months. Across a full cycle it averages ~7%.

**What do I do when the 0% ends?** Pay the balance in full from the cash you parked in savings, then move your spend to the next card whose 0% window is open.

**Are the rewards and float interest taxable?** Sign-up bonuses on spend generally aren't; the savings-account interest is (1099-INT). Set aside ~15%.
